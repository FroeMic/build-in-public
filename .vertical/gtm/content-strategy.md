# Content Strategy

*Last updated: 2026-06-08*

## Goal
Build a founder-led audience around one question:

What would it take for an agent to start and run a software business?

Do not optimize for follower count first. Optimize for becoming associated with this question among founders using coding agents, AI builders, researchers, operators, early customers, and angels.

Primary signals:
- Interesting people engaging.
- DMs from founders and builders.
- Early users asking for access.
- Angels and operators asking sharper questions.
- Repeated association between Vertical and agentic businesses.

## Audience
Primary audience:
- Technical founders using coding agents.
- Builders with products and early customers whose bottleneck is customer acquisition.
- AI-native operators and researchers thinking about agentic workflows.
- Potential angels who understand frontier software and founder-led GTM.

Secondary audience:
- Developer tool founders.
- Indie hackers and solo SaaS builders.
- People building agent infrastructure, GTM agents, and software factories.

## Content Pillars
### 1. Feedback Loops and Agentic Businesses
Own the question of why coding agents learn faster than business agents and what the business equivalent of tests could be.

Example angles:
- Software has code -> test -> result -> iterate. Companies do not.
- Business agents need to learn from outcomes, not just complete tasks.
- Product -> market -> learning -> product.

### 2. Building vs Distribution
Explore the shift from software creation as bottleneck to customer acquisition as bottleneck.

Example angles:
- Builders can now ship products faster than they can get customers.
- The temptation to keep building remains strong.
- Getting customers is where the next leverage layer is missing.

### 3. Business Context and Memory
Explain why persistent business memory matters for agents.

Example angles:
- Without persistence there is no business.
- CRM, tasks, product decisions, experiments, and revenue are different views of the same system.
- Context may be more valuable than automation.

### 4. Human-Agent Collaboration
Share observations about visibility, trust, UI, and steering agentic systems.

Example angles:
- Users still want a UI in agent-first systems.
- The challenge is not getting agents to act; it is making their work legible.
- The system should answer: what happened, why, what is next, where intervention is needed.

### 5. The Glue
Share practical observations about connecting agents to the outside world.

Example angles:
- Agents are useful in proportion to what they can touch.
- The business lives across email, CRM, GitHub, Stripe, analytics, docs, spreadsheets, and social accounts.
- Without integrations, agents become advisors. With integrations, they can become operators.
- Permissions, trust, and API messiness are product primitives.

### 6. Field Notes From Building Vertical
Use concrete learnings from users, product shifts, fundraising thinking, and team decisions.

Example angles:
- Strong conviction about direction, low conviction about product.
- Starting from zero introduced too many variables.
- Market quality matters more than product quality.
- Make it work, make it right, make it fast.

### 7. Second-Time Founder Notes
Use sparingly for observations that come from building a company the second time.

This bucket should add range, not become the center of the account. Good posts should connect back to the broader Vertical lens: markets, learning velocity, iteration, distribution, product taste, fundraising, and how founders think under uncertainty.

Example angles:
- Market quality matters more than product quality.
- The second time around, the question matters more than the first answer.
- You become less attached to ideas and more attached to learning speed.
- Some "success" can hide that you are winning in the wrong market.

## Cadence
X / Twitter:
- 1 anchor/thesis post per week.
- 3-5 short working-note posts per week.
- 2-3 field-note posts per week when there is real user/product activity.
- 10-20 thoughtful replies per day to founders, AI builders, coding-agent users, and GTM operators.

LinkedIn:
- 1-2 posts per week, usually expanded versions of the strongest X ideas.
- Keep the tone clear and thoughtful, but not corporate.

Do not force "shipping updates" before there is something real to share. Use observations, questions, and field notes while the product work catches up.

## Distribution
- Reply under people already discussing coding agents, AI founders, GTM automation, and software factories.
- Turn strong replies into standalone posts.
- Use one recurring weekly format: "Field note from building Vertical".
- Use founder voice, not brand voice.
- Use second-time founder notes occasionally, roughly 1 in 10 posts, and only when there is a sharp observation.
- Pin only when a post earns it. The first feedback-loop post is a flag-planting post, not necessarily the permanent pinned post.

## Style Calibration
Reference examples live in `.vertical/gtm/style-reference.md`.

Use the content pillars for planning, but do not let posts sound like the pillar names.

Default shape:
- Concrete observation.
- Specific example.
- Sparse contrast.
- Optional unresolved question.

Prefer:
- "Thinking about..."
- "A distinction I've been playing with..."
- "One thing that surprised me..."
- "I keep coming back to..."
- "Maybe..."

Use practical language:
- find people
- earn attention
- did they reply?
- did they buy?
- building is addictive
- the market did not get faster

Be careful with:
- "distribution"
- "outcome-based learning"
- "business workflows"
- "agentic leverage"
- "the bottleneck is moving"

These words can be useful internally, but public posts should usually translate them into lived language.

Avoid overcorrecting. The account should still have a thesis. The goal is not vagueness; it is curious precision. Prefer working observations over finished lessons unless the final line is genuinely sharp.

## Review Rhythm
Weekly:
- Review which ideas got thoughtful replies or DMs.
- Move resonant ideas into `.vertical/gtm/content-ideas.md` as follow-ups.
- Identify one idea worth expanding into a LinkedIn post or longer memo.

Monthly:
- Update the thesis based on user conversations and product evidence.
- Retire content pillars that feel abstract without field evidence.
- Promote the best-performing post into a sharper anchor post.
