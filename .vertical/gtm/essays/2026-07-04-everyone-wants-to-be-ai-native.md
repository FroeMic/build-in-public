Title: **Everyone wants to be AI-native.**

Halfway through the park, my friend stopped walking. We had been circling the same question since we picked up our coffees: what actually makes a company AI-native? Luckily, a bench was nearby. Under the shadow of a tree, we lost ourselves in it. We did not need the coffee to leave full of energy.

Startups experiment with it. VCs fund it. Consultants sell roadmaps for it. Heck, I want to build one.

But ask five people what it means and you get five answers. On that bench, the two of us had two.

The version I kept defending: an AI-native company has AI at the center. Not tools at the edges, making people faster. One shared layer that work, context, and decisions route through. Agents act from it. Humans set the direction, the taste, what to trust.

How is that different from today?

Most of what makes a company unique today is tacit. It lives between people, departments, suppliers, partners. Who knows what. How deals actually close. Why the pricing exception exists. The organization is a transactive memory system. That's the alpha most companies have.

And if your AI can't see it, your AI stays a copilot for the human who can.

Companies look the way they do because no one can hold the whole company in their head. Middle management routes information. Roles divide what one mind can't contain.

AI models can hold it. A million tokens at a time.

Capture that fullness in a machine-readable layer, and you can build different companies. Weird ones. Every employee sees the entire organization. So does every agent. Smaller, faster to learn. The reach of AI with the taste of humans.

Take one sales call. Today it ends as a note nobody reads, maybe a CRM field, maybe a Slack message. In an AI-native company it updates what the company knows: the customer, the objections, the discount they asked for. The follow-up email drafts itself. And it does not stop in sales. The objection a customer raised on Tuesday sits in front of the engineer scoping that feature on Thursday. The CEO no longer hears the market through the VP of Sales. He can ask a question across five hundred calls and set priorities from the answer.

Why doesn't it exist yet?

Tacit knowledge has resisted capture for as long as companies have existed. Everyone knows the outdated wiki. The graveyard of empty CRM fields. Writing it down and keeping it up-to-date is a tax nobody wants to pay.

But this might actually be solvable now:

AI can observe. AI can update. AI can resurface.

Capture stops depending on discipline.

So how do you build an AI-native company?

I wish I could tell you. I wish I knew myself.

I can only experiment and find out. Existing companies will have to rethink how their core processes work. Startups get to think from scratch.

We start like this: everything my co-founder and I do goes into gbrain.

The next step: agents that act on that context, and learn from what goes wrong.

Nothing else in a company works like that. Sales doesn't get better when a model ships. Support doesn't. Hiring doesn't. Someone has to notice, learn, and rewire — every time.

That's the difference that matters. AI at the edges makes people faster today. AI at the center means the company itself improves every time the models do. One is a boost. The other compounds.

Nobody has built the compounding version yet. The startups experimenting are guessing. So are we. The tech isn't fully there. The org patterns aren't either.

Everyone wants to be AI-native. It doesn't exist yet.
