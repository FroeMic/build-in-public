# Taste

What Micha's edits reveal about how he wants to sound. Every rule here is
evidenced by a real draft→final pair. Updated by `/capture` after each
published post. Keep this file under ~150 lines: merge and sharpen rules
instead of appending forever. When a new pair contradicts a rule, flag it —
taste is allowed to change.

## Rules

1. **Lowercase, no terminal punctuation on X.** Questions lose their question
   marks too. On LinkedIn: keep capitalized "I" and light punctuation, but the
   same casual register. (evidence: every pair below)

2. **Open with a working-note frame, not a thesis.** "thinking about...",
   "question i keep coming back to", "one thing i keep noticing", "the more i
   build with agents the more i think...". The draft versions opened with
   declarations ("Without persistence, there is no business.") — all got cut.

3. **No colon-then-list setups.** Never "It lives across:" — just state the
   frame, then bare lines. Lists are unpunctuated single lines.

4. **Abstract nouns become lived phrasing.** "persistence" → "a company is
   mostly memory"; "operational memory" → "who we talked to / what they said /
   what we tried"; "Permissions become important. Audit trails become
   important." → "permissions / audit logs / undo / review / credentials".
   If a word belongs in a strategy doc, it doesn't belong in the post.

5. **Cut the lesson.** Lines that explain why the point matters get deleted
   ("This is why integrations matter so much for business agents."). End on
   the sharpest concrete line or the small insight, not a summary
   ("it is just helping with tasks", "autonomy is hard to trust when you
   cannot see the work").

6. **Hedge like a person thinking, not a brand asserting.** "mostly",
   "probably", "i think", "may". Drafted confidence ("Feels like the answer
   says a lot") became "the answer probably says a lot" — tighter AND softer.

7. **Fewer, more specific list items.** Tool lists got real: "CRM, GitHub,
   Stripe, social accounts" → "github / linear / stripe / slack / internal
   apis". Name the actual tools in the stack, not the category exemplars.

8. **Observations, never advice — including in essays.** No commands, no
   second-person instruction ("Stop adding AI" — rejected), no opening that
   addresses the reader's behavior. Credibility comes from having interesting
   views and field access, not from convincing. When borrowing an author's
   structure (e.g. Sivers), take the compression and shape; the stance stays
   Micha's: share what was seen and thought, let the reader draw the
   conclusion. Authority may be claimed only from what he actually did or
   heard firsthand. (evidence: 2026-07-04 essay-structure session)

## Cut list

Phrases and shapes that got edited out. Never draft these:

- Opening thesis declarations ("X, there is no Y.")
- "That sounds obvious, but..."
- "One thing I underestimated:" (became "one thing i keep noticing")
- Setup colons before lists
- Closing summary/lesson lines
- "become important" repetition patterns
- Question marks on rhetorical questions (X only)
- Endings that reveal product motivation ("That's why I want to build it" —
  rejected 2026-07-04: turns a shared thought into a pitch. Ending on one's
  own concrete practice is fine; ending on wanting/selling is not.)

## Calibration pairs

Real examples. Match the *final*, not the draft.

### Pair 1 — persistence post (X, 2026-06-15)

Draft:
```
Without persistence, there is no business.

An agent cannot operate a company if it forgets:

customers
conversations
experiments
revenue
tasks
product decisions

The interesting memory is not abstract.

It is the operational memory of what happened, what changed, and what should happen next.
```

Final:
```
thinking about business agents today

a company is mostly memory

who we talked to
what they said
what we tried
what worked
what changed
what we promised
what we should do next

if the agent forgets that every time it is not really operating the business

it is just helping with tasks
```

### Pair 2 — users want UI (X, 2026-06-18)

Draft:
```
One thing I underestimated:

Even in agent-first products, users still want a UI.

Not because they want more buttons.

Because they want to understand:

what happened
why it happened
what happens next
where they need to intervene

Autonomy without legibility is hard to trust.
```

Final:
```
one thing i keep noticing

even people who want agents still want a ui

not because they want more buttons

because they want to know

what happened
why it happened
what happens next
where they need to intervene

autonomy is hard to trust when you cannot see the work
```

### Pair 3 — advisors vs operators (X, 2026-06-17)

Draft:
```
Without integrations, agents become advisors.

With integrations, they can become operators.

That sounds obvious, but it changes the product surface.

Permissions become important.
Audit trails become important.
Recovery becomes important.
Trust becomes important.

The hard part is not just intelligence.

It is safe access to the outside world.
```

Final:
```
without integrations agents are mostly advisors

with integrations they can start to operate

but then the product problem changes

permissions
audit logs
undo
review
credentials
who can call what

safe access to the outside world becomes the hard part
```
