# Style pack: Derek Sivers

Compressed writing instructions for sive.rs-style short essays. Use for
long-form: LinkedIn posts, memos, future blog essays. NOT a replacement for
`TASTE.md` — Micha's X voice hedges ("probably", "i think") where Derek
asserts. When drafting for X, TASTE.md wins. This pack is a practice tool and
a long-form register.

Grounded in: sive.rs/1s (his own method, quoted below), sive.rs/obvious,
sive.rs/donkey, sive.rs/hellyeah.

## The shape

- **Title is the idea.** "Obvious to you. Amazing to others." The essay
  delivers the title; it doesn't tease it.
- **Open mid-scene or with a question to the reader.** "Any creator of
  anything knows this feeling:" / "Are you trying to pursue many different
  directions at once?" No windup, no context-setting.
- **One idea per essay.** Story or scene → one turn → the principle. The turn
  is a single contrast ("Then someone told me my work amazed them").
- **End on a command or aphorism. Never a summary.** "We should just put them
  out there and let the world decide." / "Think long term. Use the future.
  Don't be short sighted. Don't be a donkey."
- **200–400 words.** If it's longer, it's two essays.

## The method (his own words, from sive.rs/1s)

Write one sentence per line, for your eyes only:

> It helps you judge each sentence on its own. We sometimes write sentences
> that don't need to exist. Hidden in a paragraph, we might not notice.
> Standing on their own, we notice. Delete any sentence not worthy of its own
> line.
>
> First words punch. Last words linger. Chop the weak beginnings, like
> "I think" and "Whether or not". Start with powerful subjects and verbs.

Draft this way, then join into paragraphs at the end.

## Sentence mechanics

- 8–15 words average. Vary hard: "Sometimes short. Sometimes long."
- One thought per sentence. One to three sentences per paragraph.
- Plain words a 12-year-old knows. No jargon, no latinate abstractions.
- Active voice, present tense, strong verbs. Almost no adjectives or adverbs.
- Second person, direct: "You can do everything you want to do."
- Questions as pivots: "Why is it so useful?"
- Assertions, not hedges. Derek deletes "I think" — he states it.
- Real specifics: his own numbers, named moments, one vivid parable
  (Buridan's donkey) rather than three weak examples.

## Anti-patterns (instant fails)

- Throat-clearing openings ("In this post...", "I've been thinking about...")
- Caveats and qualifier stacks ("somewhat", "arguably", "in many cases")
- Explaining the point after making it
- Summary/recap endings
- More than one idea
- Cleverness that draws attention to the writing instead of the idea

## Calibration lines

Match these, not a description of them:

- Opening: "Any creator of anything knows this feeling:"
- Turn: "Everybody's ideas seem obvious to them."
- Principle: "If you're not saying 'HELL YEAH!' about something, say no."
- Ending: "We should just put them out there and let the world decide."
- Ending (staccato): "Think long term. Use the future. Don't be short
  sighted. Don't be a donkey."
